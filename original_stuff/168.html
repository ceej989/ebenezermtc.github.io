                                                       Cross the symbol of unfailing love

 

Jean Valjean, a convict on parole, coming to a town where the only person who’ll give him a room and a meal is the local bishop, Bp Myriel. The bishop welcomes him into his house, feeds him and gives him a warm bed for the night. However, the riches of the bishop’s house prove too much for Jean Valjean. He succumbs to temptation and steals the bishop’s silver cutlery. He’s soon apprehended, though, and when the gendarmes bring him back, the bishop is faced with a dilemma: should he tell the truth, that Jean Valjean has stolen the silver, in which case he’ll be thrown back into prison, or should he show him the mercy of the gospel and make out that it was given to him as a gift? The choice he has to make is between mercy and justice. He can’t have both. Well, as you probably know, he chooses mercy. He lies to the gendarmes. He says that the silver was a gift. In fact he insists that Jean Valjean takes not just the silver cutlery, but his silver candlesticks as well. But before Jean Valjean leaves, he’s told that this gift of love requires a response. From now on he must reform his life, from now on he must begin to live as a law-abiding citizen. 

What is the story of the cross? Why do we observe cross as the symbol of Christian spirituality?

We are once again in the Lenten season where we observe the passion of our Lord Jesus. Jesus gave us this beautiful symbol as the centre of our spirituality which always challenges us with the unfailing and unfathomable love of God, which he expresses through the cross. In the Old Testament we see a God who accompanies a murderer, who hears the cry of the little child in the wilderness, who loves his people who always prostituted their master, who marries an unfaithful wife, and finally sending his own son to die to conquer death. This love continues through the symbol of the cross. Once again we are meditating on this unfailing love which comes from the everlasting fountain of the cross of Jesus and that is our hope in this world of individualism and ‘lovelessness’. The one thing that takes us forward in the world of hatred, retaliation, jealousy and selfishness is the love that flows from this fountain.

If God loves us like this, what should be our response to this cross? The hymn writer Franc­es Hav­er­gal is asking these words to us

“I gave My life for thee, My precious blood I shed,
That thou might ransomed be, and raised up from the dead
I gave, I gave My life for thee, what hast thou given for Me?
I gave, I gave My life for thee, what hast thou given for Me?”

What is our response? Let this season of prayer and lent help us to fathom this fountain of love. Let this season takes us more closer to Him.

 

V. M. Mathew achen

Youth Chaplain
